📚 How It Works

Planning for Retirement

Retirement planning is about estimating your future needs and working backwards to determine your required savings rate. The 4% rule and compound growth are your best tools.

📐 The 4% Rule
Retirement Savings = Annual Expenses × 25
4% Rule: Withdraw 4% of savings in year 1
Adjust annually: For inflation (historically 3%)
Success rate: 95% over 30 years historically
Conservative: Use 3.5% for early retirement
Example: If you need $60,000/year, target $1.5M (60k × 25). Withdraw $60k in year 1, then adjust for inflation each year.
🎯 Retirement Income Sources
  • ✓ 401(k) / 403(b) — Employer-sponsored, tax-deferred growth
  • ✓ Traditional IRA — Tax deduction now, taxed in retirement
  • ✓ Roth IRA — Tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals
  • ✓ Social Security — Starts at 62-70, average $1,800/month
  • ✓ Pension — Guaranteed income if offered by employer
  • ✓ Taxable Investments — Flexibility, capital gains rates

❓ FAQ

Common questions

Use the 25x rule: Multiply your desired annual retirement income by 25. If you need $60,000/year, aim for $1.5 million. This follows the 4% safe withdrawal rate — you can withdraw 4% in year 1 ($60,000), then adjust for inflation annually. If retiring early (before 60), use 30x expenses (3.3% rule) for extra safety. Social Security and pensions reduce the amount you need to save yourself.
You can retire when your savings can sustainably cover expenses. Full Social Security starts at 67 (born after 1960). Medicare begins at 65. Many aim for financial independence where work becomes optional — often in their 50s or early 60s with aggressive saving (FIRE movement). The key factor isn't age, but having 25-30× your annual expenses saved. Some retire at 40 with $1M+ saved; others work into their 70s by choice.
Rule of thumb: 15% of gross income starting at age 25. If starting later: Age 30 = 18%, Age 35 = 23%, Age 40 = 30%, Age 45 = 40%. Max out employer 401(k) match first (free money), then fill Roth IRA ($7,000/year limit), then back to 401(k) ($23,000/year limit). Use this calculator to find your exact number based on your goal age and desired income. Even $200/month starting at 25 grows to $500k+ by 65.
401(k): Tax deduction now, taxed in retirement. Best if current tax rate > expected retirement tax rate. Employer match is free money — always max this first. Roth IRA: No deduction now, tax-free growth and withdrawals. Best for young/low-income workers (low tax rate now) or if you expect higher taxes later. Ideal strategy: 401(k) to get full match → Roth IRA max ($7,000) → Back to 401(k) max ($23,000) → Taxable brokerage. Diversifying tax treatments gives flexibility in retirement.
Social Security replaces about 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners. The average monthly benefit is $1,800 (2024), maximum is about $4,800 for those who maxed out contributions for 35 years. Claim anytime from 62-70: Age 62 = 70% of full benefit, Age 67 = 100% (full retirement age), Age 70 = 124% of full benefit. Use ssa.gov for your exact estimate. Benefits are adjusted for inflation annually (COLA).
Historical inflation averages 3% annually. The 4% rule already accounts for inflation — you withdraw 4% of the initial balance in year 1, then increase that dollar amount by inflation each subsequent year. Your investments should grow faster than inflation over time (historically 7% real returns for stocks). However, plan for healthcare costs which inflate faster (5-6%). Consider delaying Social Security to age 70 for maximum inflation-adjusted income. Keep 1-2 years of expenses in cash/CDs to avoid selling investments during market downturns.
FIRE (Financial Independence Retire Early) requires aggressive saving: 50-70% of income for 10-20 years. Target is 25-30× annual expenses. Lean FIRE: Minimal expenses, retire with $500k-1M. Fat FIRE: $5M+ for luxury lifestyle. Barista FIRE: Part-time work covers expenses, investments grow untouched. Keys to success: High income, low expenses, aggressive investing (mostly index funds), and planning for healthcare (ACA subsidies, HSAs). The "RE" in FIRE is optional — many achieve financial independence but keep working by choice.
