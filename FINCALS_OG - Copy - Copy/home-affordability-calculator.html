📚 How It Works

The 28/36 Rule Explained

Lenders use the 28/36 rule to determine how much you can borrow. It keeps your housing costs manageable while leaving room for other expenses and savings.

📐 The 28/36 Rule
28% = Housing Costs / Gross Monthly Income
28% Rule (Front-End DTI): Max for housing only
36% Rule (Back-End DTI): Max for all debts combined
Housing Costs: PITI + HOA fees
All Debts: Housing + car + student + credit cards
Conservative approach: Some buyers prefer 25% for housing to leave more room for savings and unexpected expenses.
🏠 What Affects Affordability
  • ✓ Credit Score — Higher scores get better rates (saves thousands)
  • ✓ Down Payment — 20% avoids PMI, but 3.5-10% works with FHA/conventional
  • ✓ Debt-to-Income — Lower existing debt = more house you can afford
  • ✓ Interest Rates — 1% rate change affects buying power by ~10%
  • ✓ Property Taxes — Vary wildly by location ($500-$12,000/year)
  • ✓ HOA Fees — Can add $100-500/month to housing costs

❓ FAQ

Common questions

Use the 28/36 rule: Spend no more than 28% of gross monthly income on housing (PITI + HOA), and 36% on total debt (housing + car + student loans + credit cards). For example, with $6,250/month income ($75k/year), max housing is $1,750/month. If you have $500 in other debt payments, your total debt limit is $2,250/month, leaving $1,750 for housing (the 28% rule governs). Your actual maximum depends on down payment, interest rate, property taxes, and existing debts.
DTI compares monthly debt payments to gross monthly income. Front-end DTI (housing costs only): Should be under 28%. Back-end DTI (all debts): Should be under 36% for conventional loans, though FHA allows up to 43% and some lenders accept 50% with strong credit and reserves. Calculate: DTI = (Monthly Debt Payments / Gross Monthly Income) × 100. Lower DTI = better loan terms and more breathing room in your budget.
Yes, if applying jointly. Combined income increases buying power significantly. Both credit scores matter too — lenders typically use the lower middle score of both applicants. If one spouse has poor credit (under 620), consider applying alone to get better rates, but then only that person's income counts. Important: Both spouses are equally responsible for the debt on a joint mortgage, even if only one works. Consider life and disability insurance to protect the surviving spouse's ability to keep the home.
No. The 28% rule covers PITI + HOA only: Principal, Interest, Taxes, Insurance, and HOA fees. Utilities (electric, gas, water, internet) are additional costs typically running $200-500/month depending on home size and climate. Many financial advisors recommend budgeting 30-32% of income for total housing costs including utilities. When comparing renting vs buying, remember renters often pay lower utilities (smaller spaces, shared walls) while homeowners face all utility costs plus maintenance (1-3% of home value annually).
High student loans reduce home affordability because they count in your 36% back-end DTI. Strategies: 1) Income-driven repayment — FHA lenders may use lower IDR payment instead of standard 1% of balance. 2) Pay down debt first — Eliminating $400/month debt increases buying power by ~$75,000. 3) Increase income — Side hustle or promotion improves DTI ratio. 4) Consider FHA loans — Allow 43% DTI vs 36% conventional. 5) Co-signer — Parent's income (but not debt) can help qualify, though they're equally responsible. Don't rush into homeownership if debt-burdened — renting and building emergency savings first is often wiser.
Interest rates dramatically impact buying power. At 6.5% on a $300k home, monthly P&I is ~$1,896. At 7.5%, it's ~$2,097 — $201 more per month. To keep the same $1,896 payment at 7.5%, you can only afford a ~$272k home. Rule of thumb: 1% rate increase reduces buying power by ~10%. This is why buyers rush when rates drop and pause when rates rise. Consider: buying at higher rates with plans to refinance when rates fall, or buying a less expensive home to keep payments manageable. Always get pre-approved to know your exact rate and buying power.
Generally no. Buying at your absolute maximum leaves no room for: emergencies (roof leak, HVAC failure), lifestyle changes (new baby, job loss), or savings (retirement, vacation). Many regret being "house poor." Better approach: Target 20-25% of income for housing, not 28%. On $75k income, that's $1,250-1,562/month instead of $1,750. This buffer allows you to: build emergency fund, save for maintenance, enjoy life, and sleep better. Remember: your lender's max is based on math, not your happiness or financial security. Buy what you can comfortably afford, not what you can barely qualify for.
