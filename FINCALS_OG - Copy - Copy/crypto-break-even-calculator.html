📚 How It Works

Understanding Break-Even Price

Your break-even price is the minimum sell price required to recover your initial investment after paying exchange fees on both the buy and sell sides.

📐 The Formula
Break-Even = Buy Price × (1 + Buy Fee%) ÷ (1 - Sell Fee%)
Buy Price = Price per coin when purchasing
Buy Fee% = Exchange fee as decimal (0.001 = 0.1%)
Sell Fee% = Exchange fee as decimal (0.001 = 0.1%)
Result = Minimum sell price for zero profit/loss
Why this works: You pay fees when buying (reducing coins received) and when selling (reducing proceeds). The formula accounts for both fee impacts to find your true break-even point.
🎯 Why This Matters
  • ✓ Avoid surprise losses — Know your minimum profitable exit before trading
  • ✓ Compare exchanges — Higher fees mean higher break-even prices
  • ✓ Plan your trades — Set stop-losses and take-profits with fee costs included
  • ✓ Tax planning — Break-even is your cost basis for capital gains calculations

❓ FAQ

Common questions

The crypto break-even price is the exact sell price at which you recover your initial investment plus all trading fees, resulting in zero profit and zero loss. It accounts for both the buy fee (which reduces how many coins you receive) and the sell fee (which reduces your proceeds when exiting the trade).
Check your exchange's fee schedule in your account settings or trading rules. Common rates: Binance (0.1% standard), Coinbase Pro (0.5% for low volume), Kraken (0.16-0.26%), and decentralized exchanges (0.3% or variable gas fees). Fees often decrease with higher trading volume or when using exchange tokens (like BNB on Binance).
Your break-even price is always higher than your buy price because you pay fees on both sides of the trade. The buy fee reduces the amount of cryptocurrency you receive, and the sell fee reduces the cash you get when selling. For example, with 0.1% fees on both sides, you need approximately 0.2% price appreciation just to break even.
This calculator focuses on exchange trading fees (maker/taker fees). Network or gas fees for blockchain transfers are separate and vary by network congestion. If you're moving crypto between wallets or exchanges, add those costs to your investment amount or consider them as additional overhead in your trading strategy.
Maker fees are charged when you add liquidity to the order book (placing limit orders that don't fill immediately). Taker fees are charged when you remove liquidity (market orders or limit orders that fill immediately). Makers typically pay lower fees (0-0.1%) while takers pay standard rates (0.1-0.5%). Use maker orders when possible to reduce your break-even price.
This calculator is designed for spot trading (buying actual cryptocurrency). For leveraged trading, you must also account for funding rates, margin interest, and liquidation prices. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses, and fees are typically charged on the total position size, not just your collateral. Use specialized leverage calculators for margin trading break-even calculations.
