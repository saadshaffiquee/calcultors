📚 How It Works

The Power of Extra Payments

Making extra principal payments creates a snowball effect. Every dollar you pay extra reduces the balance that accrues interest, so more of your next regular payment goes to principal instead of interest.

📐 How Extra Payments Work
New Balance = Old Balance - (Payment - Interest)
Interest = Balance × (Rate ÷ 12)
Principal = Payment - Interest
With Extra: Principal = (Payment + Extra) - Interest
The snowball effect: Lower balance → Less interest → More principal paid → Even lower balance next month
💡 Debt Payoff Strategies
  • ✓ Avalanche Method — Pay extra on highest interest debt first (saves most money)
  • ✓ Snowball Method — Pay extra on smallest balance first (best for motivation)
  • ✓ Biweekly Payments — Pay half monthly amount every 2 weeks (13 full payments/year)
  • ✓ Round Up — Round payments to nearest $50 or $100
  • ✓ Windfalls — Apply tax refunds, bonuses, gifts directly to principal

❓ FAQ

Common questions

Extra payments go directly to principal, immediately reducing your balance. Next month, interest is calculated on this lower balance, so less of your regular payment goes to interest and more to principal. This creates a compounding snowball effect: each month you pay extra, the benefit grows. On a typical loan, $100 extra in month 1 saves you $1-2 in interest; by month 50, that same $100 saves $3-4 because the balance is lower.
Compare your loan rate to expected investment returns. High-interest debt (8%+ like credit cards): Pay off aggressively — guaranteed 20%+ return. Medium rates (4-7% like student loans): Split decision — many choose 50/50 between extra payments and investing. Low rates (under 4%): Invest instead — historical market returns average 7-10%. Also consider: debt payoff is a guaranteed return, reduces monthly stress, and improves credit. Investing has tax advantages (401k match, Roth IRA) and compounds over decades.
Mathematically: Pay highest interest first (Avalanche Method) — saves the most money. Psychologically: Pay smallest balance first (Snowball Method) — quick wins build momentum. Hybrid approach: Pay off any loan under $1,000 first for the psychological win, then switch to highest interest. Also consider: loans with cosigners (free them), variable rate loans (rising rates), or loans with annoying servicers. The best method is the one you'll actually stick with!
Federal student loans: Never have prepayment penalties. Auto loans: Rarely have penalties anymore, but check your contract. Mortgages: Most don't, but some have penalties in first 3-5 years. Personal loans: Varies by lender — some charge 1-2% of remaining balance. Credit cards: Never have prepayment penalties. Always check your loan agreement's "prepayment" or "early payoff" section. If there's a penalty, calculate if the interest savings still make early payoff worthwhile.
Biweekly payments are a powerful "hidden" extra payment strategy. You pay half your monthly amount every 2 weeks. Since there are 52 weeks in a year, you make 26 half-payments = 13 full payments instead of 12. That's one extra payment per year without feeling the pinch! On a $200k mortgage at 6%, biweekly payments save about $30,000 in interest and pay off the loan 4-5 years early. Many lenders offer biweekly options, or you can set up automatic transfers yourself.
Yes, absolutely. Always have at least a $1,000 "starter" emergency fund before aggressive debt payoff. Otherwise, any surprise (car repair, medical bill, job loss) forces you back into debt. Ideal order: 1) Pay minimums on all debts, 2) Build $1,000 emergency fund, 3) Pay off high-interest debt (10%+), 4) Build 3-6 month emergency fund, 5) Pay off medium-interest debt (4-9%), 6) Invest for retirement. The emergency fund prevents the "pay off $500, borrow $600" cycle that keeps people trapped in debt.
Most modern lenders automatically apply extra payments to principal, but always verify. When making an online payment, look for "principal only" option. If mailing a check, write "Apply to principal" in the memo. Call your servicer to confirm their policy. Some shady lenders apply extra to future payments (which doesn't save interest) instead of principal. If this happens, call and demand a reallocation. Keep records of all principal-only payments and check your balance the following month to confirm it dropped correctly.
