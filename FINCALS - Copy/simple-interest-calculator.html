📚 How It Works

Understanding Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal amount. Unlike compound interest, it doesn't grow exponentially — it grows linearly.

📐 The Formula
A = P(1 + RT)
A = Total Amount (principal + interest)
P = Principal amount (initial sum)
R = Annual interest rate (in decimal)
T = Time (in years)
Or separately: Interest I = P × R × T, then Total A = P + I
🎯 When to Use Simple Interest
  • ✓ Short-term loans — Personal loans under 1 year
  • ✓ Car loans — Many auto loans use simple interest
  • ✓ Student loans — Some federal loans calculate this way
  • ✓ Basic savings — Some traditional savings accounts
  • ✓ Personal lending — Loans between friends/family

❓ FAQ

Common questions

Simple interest is interest calculated only on the original principal amount. It does not compound, meaning you don't earn (or pay) interest on previously accumulated interest. The formula is A = P(1 + RT) for total amount, or I = P × R × T for interest only.
Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount and grows linearly. Compound interest is calculated on principal plus accumulated interest, growing exponentially. For example, $10,000 at 5% for 3 years: Simple interest total = $11,500. Compound interest total (annual) = $11,576. The gap widens dramatically over longer periods.
Simple interest is common for short-term loans (under 1 year), many car loans, some student loans, and basic savings accounts. Compound interest is used for most investments, credit cards, mortgages, and long-term savings accounts. Always check your loan documents to see which method applies.
For the formula, time must be in years. Convert months by dividing by 12 (6 months = 0.5 years). Convert days by dividing by 365 (90 days = 90/365 = 0.2466 years). Our calculator handles this conversion automatically when you select your time unit.
Simple interest is generally better for borrowers because total interest is lower than compound interest for the same rate and term. For lenders/savers, compound interest is better because you earn more. However, simple interest loans often have higher rates to compensate, so always compare the total cost.
This calculator provides mathematically accurate simple interest calculations. However, real loans may include fees, prepayment penalties, or different day-count conventions (360 vs 365 days). Always verify with your lender's disclosure documents before signing.
