📚 How It Works

Understanding Compound Interest

Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. It creates exponential growth because you earn interest on your interest, not just the original amount.

📐 The Formula
A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
A = Future value (total amount)
P = Principal (initial investment)
r = Annual interest rate (decimal)
n = Compounding frequency per year
t = Time in years
With contributions: Each monthly payment also compounds, creating even more growth over time.
🎯 Why Compound Interest Matters
  • ✓ Exponential growth — Your money grows faster over time
  • ✓ Passive income — Money makes money while you sleep
  • ✓ Retirement planning — Small contributions grow to large sums
  • ✓ Beat inflation — Grow purchasing power over decades
  • ✓ Wealth building — The foundation of long-term investing

❓ FAQ

Common questions

Compound interest is interest calculated on your initial principal plus all accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest, you earn "interest on interest," creating exponential growth. The formula is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where n is how many times per year interest compounds.
The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn. Daily compounding > Monthly > Quarterly > Annually. However, the difference is usually small. For example, $10,000 at 7% for 20 years: Annual compounding = $38,697, Monthly = $40,275, Daily = $40,552. Monthly is typically the sweet spot for most investments.
APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the simple annual rate without compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) includes compounding and shows your actual return. For example, 7% APR compounded monthly = 7.23% APY. Banks usually advertise APY for savings accounts because it's higher and more accurate.
Use our calculator to experiment! As a rule of thumb: To reach $1 million in 30 years at 7% return, you need to save about $800/month starting from zero. But starting with $10,000 and adding $500/month gets you to $1.1 million in 30 years. The earlier you start, the less you need to save monthly thanks to compounding.
For savers and investors, absolutely yes. Compound interest dramatically outperforms simple interest over time. For example, $10,000 at 7% for 20 years: Simple interest = $24,000 total. Compound interest (monthly) = $40,275 total. That's a $16,275 difference! However, if you're borrowing, simple interest is better for you.
This calculator shows mathematical projections. Real investments (stocks, bonds, crypto) can lose value and don't guarantee returns. However, FDIC-insured savings accounts and CDs do offer guaranteed compound interest, though at lower rates (currently 4-5% APY). Always consider risk tolerance and diversify investments.
The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math trick: Divide 72 by your interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money. At 7% interest, 72 ÷ 7 = ~10.3 years to double. At 10%, it's 7.2 years. It's surprisingly accurate for rates between 6% and 10%.
